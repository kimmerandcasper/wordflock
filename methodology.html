We believe in conducting political investigations into voter issues utilizing the scientific method outlined as follows:

Ask a Question
The process starts with a curious observation leading to a question about what has been observed. The question should be specific, measurable, and definable.
Example: Why do certain plants grow faster in sunlight than in shade?

Do Background Research
Research what is already known about the topic. This helps refine the question and ensures that the experiment adds to the existing body of knowledge rather than replicating past research.
Example: Study existing literature on photosynthesis, light, and plant growth.

Construct a Hypothesis
A hypothesis is an educated guess about how things work. It should be a testable statement that addresses the original question. The hypothesis often takes the form of a cause-and-effect statement that can be disproved or supported by experimental data.
Example: Plants exposed to more sunlight grow faster than those exposed to less sunlight because sunlight increases the rate of photosynthesis.

Test Your Hypothesis by Doing an Experiment
Design an experiment to test whether the hypothesis is valid. This involves planning how to manipulate the variables and defining control and experimental groups.
Example: Set up two sets of the same species of plants, one exposed to sunlight for 12 hours a day and the other exposed to only 4 hours of sunlight.

Analyze Your Data and Draw a Conclusion
Once the experiment is complete, collect and analyze the data to determine if it supports or contradicts your hypothesis. Statistical analysis can often be involved in this step to interpret the results.
Example: Measure the growth of plants over a specified period, and use statistical methods to compare the growth rates between the two groups.

Report Your Results
Prepare a detailed report that includes the methodology, results, and conclusions. If the results align with your hypothesis, you can propose a theory based on your findings. If the hypothesis is falsified, the results should still be reported because negative results also contribute to scientific knowledge.
Example: Write a research paper outlining your experimental setup, data, statistical analysis, and conclusions about the impact of sunlight on plant growth.

Replication
Other scientists should be able to replicate your experiment under the same conditions and achieve the same results. This helps validate the findings and ensures the reliability of the scientific conclusions.
